His Work...

Instrumentum Artithmeticum

Leibniz's calculator was the first machine with which one could not only add and subtract - but also multiply and divide numbers.

Leibniz faced engineering and production difficulties and despite over forty years of design work and in today's dollars over a million spent, Leibniz machine failed to carry 10s correctly.

Stepped Reckoner

His innovation known as the stepped reckoner or Leibniz wheel was a drum with varying length teeth.

...the next 300 years

His wheel would continue to be used in mechanical calculators for three centuries from the Arithometer to the Curta Computer until the introduction of the electronic calculator in the mid-1970s.

Binary Calculations

Leibniz interest in mechanical calculation led to his further development of binary calculation as an easier method of mechanical computing.

In De progression Dyadica Leibniz describes a binary calculating machine:

This [binary] calculus could be implemented by a machine (without wheels)… provided with holes in such a way that they can be opened and closed. They are to be open at those places that correspond to a 1 and remain closed at those that correspond to a 0. Through the opened gates small cubes or marbles are to fall into tracks, through the others nothing. It [the gate array] is to be shifted from column to column as required…

Logic and Language

He also dedicated his life to the development of symbolic logic and creation of a "universal language". Described as the most important mind in logic since Aristotle and until Boole, Leibniz sought to create an algebra of human thought:

Every misunderstanding should be nothing more than a miscalculation… easily corrected by the grammatical laws of that new language. Thus in the case of a controversial discussion, two philosophers could sit down at a table and just calculating, like two mathematicians, they could say, 'Let us calculate'.

Calculus

By 1690, Leibniz had distilled most of the ideas covered in modern calculus textbooks and we still use the notation style that Leibniz devised. Newton's unwillingness to publish Leibniz's more practical notation cost English mathematicians nearly a century of isolation.
